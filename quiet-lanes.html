🌿 Walking in North Norfolk

Norfolk Quiet Lanes:
the hidden back roads

The lanes that slow you down — in the very best way

North Norfolk has a secret that the coast path crowds rarely discover. Tucked inland from the cliffs and marshes, a network of narrow country lanes winds through farming villages and ancient hedgerows, carrying almost no traffic and rewarding those on foot with the kind of unhurried quiet that is increasingly hard to find.

These are Norfolk's designated Quiet Lanes — a scheme that began here as one of the earliest of its kind in the country.

How to spot a Quiet Lane

Entry points are marked with a small dark green sign on a wooden post, showing silhouettes of a walker, cyclist and horse rider. The signs are deliberately modest — easy to miss if you're not looking, but unmistakeable once you know what you're looking for. You'll typically find them at road junctions where the lane begins.

Recognising a Quiet Lane

Quiet Lane entry points are marked with a distinctive dark green sign showing three figures: a walker, a cyclist and a horse rider. You will find these at the start of each designated lane — typically on a small post at the road junction.

The signs are deliberately understated — the ethos of the scheme is that a Quiet Lane should look like a quiet lane, not a car park.

What Are Quiet Lanes?

Quiet Lanes are officially designated minor rural roads where motorists are encouraged to take particular care around walkers, cyclists and horse riders. The concept was an initiative of the Countryside Agency, supported by the Department for Transport, and Norfolk was among the first counties in Britain to introduce the scheme.

The lanes are not closed to vehicles — they remain shared roads — but their designation shifts the balance back in favour of people on foot. Entry and exit points are clearly signed, and the routes link with footpaths, bridleways and long-distance trails across the area.

Key Fact

Norfolk was one of the first counties in Britain to run a Quiet Lanes pilot. The scheme has its roots in the late 1990s and was formalised under the Transport Act 2000.

Where Are the Quiet Lanes in North Norfolk?

The main concentration of designated Quiet Lanes forms a rough triangle of North East Norfolk — from Cromer down along the coast to Bacton, then inland to North Walsham. Around 36 miles of lanes in total, they are not a single continuous route but a network of connected roads that walkers can string together into their own circuits.

The area around Knapton, Northrepps, Southrepps, Trunch and Gimingham sits at the heart of this network — a gently rolling inland landscape of arable fields, old churchyards and brick-and-flint villages that feels entirely removed from the busier coastal roads nearby.

Walking the Lanes

The Quiet Lanes suit walkers looking for something different from the coast path. On the Norfolk Coast Path you share the route with dozens of others; on the quiet lanes, you may walk for an hour and see only a tractor and a dog walker.

The terrain is gently undulating — this part of Norfolk sits on low chalk ridges and the lanes rise and dip between hedged field edges and open farm tracks. Walking is easy underfoot on made road surfaces, though lanes can be muddy at field gateways in wet weather.

Linking the Quiet Lanes to Longer Routes

The Paston Way — a 32-kilometre trail linking Cromer with North Walsham through a chain of medieval churches — passes through the heart of Quiet Lanes country. It follows many of the same back roads between Northrepps, Southrepps, Trunch, Gimingham and Mundesley, making it the most logical ready-made itinerary for the area.

The Quiet Lanes also connect with Bacton Woods to the south, where waymarked woodland trails offer a contrast to open lane walking. Bittern Line stations at North Walsham, Gunton and Cromer provide useful start and finish points, allowing you to walk one way and take the train back.

What to Look For Along the Way

The hedgerows in this part of Norfolk are old — in some cases centuries old — and support rich wildlife through the seasons. In spring the verges are thick with cow parsley and red campion; in summer look for yellowhammers and whitethroats singing from the top of the hedges. Autumn brings fieldfares and redwings moving through berry-laden hawthorns.

Churches on the Route

The Paston Way was designed around the medieval parish churches of the area. Trunch has one of the finest font canopies in England. Knapton's church contains an extraordinary double hammerbeam roof with 160 carved angels. These are not tourist attractions in the usual sense — they are quietly magnificent, and often unlocked during daylight hours.

Practical Note

The Quiet Lanes are shared roads. Walk in single file on bends, wear something visible in low light, and give way to farm vehicles. In harvest season (July–September) expect occasional waits at field gateways.

Planning a Quiet Lanes Walk

The OS Explorer map sheet 252 (Norfolk Coast East) covers the core Quiet Lanes area and is the most useful paper map for the zone. The Paston Way leaflet — available from local tourist offices and as a download from Norfolk County Council — provides a ready-made signed itinerary using many of the same lanes.

For a straightforward introduction, a linear walk from North Walsham station to Mundesley using the Paston Way — roughly 8 miles — takes in the best of the inland lanes before finishing at the coast. The Coasthopper bus provides a simple return.

Getting There Without a Car

The Bittern Line runs from Norwich to Sheringham via North Walsham, Gunton and Cromer — all useful access points. The Coasthopper bus connects Cromer and the coastal villages. Using public transport to reach the lanes is genuinely practical and avoids the problem of returning to a parked car.

Quiet Lanes Beyond the Triangle

While the designated Quiet Lanes are concentrated in the North Walsham–Cromer–Bacton triangle, North Norfolk has lanes throughout the area that carry minimal traffic and offer equally rewarding walking — particularly the network of back roads between Burnham Market, Burnham Thorpe and the coastal villages to the north, and the lanes around Stiffkey and Cockthorpe further west.

Safety on Country Lanes

  • Walk facing oncoming traffic on single-track roads (right-hand side in the UK)
  • Move to the verge on bends and at pinch points — give farm vehicles room to pass
  • Wear bright or reflective clothing, especially on overcast days or near dusk
  • Tell someone your planned route if walking alone in remote areas
  • Check the OS map before you go — some lanes connect to busy A or B roads
  • In summer, grass verges can conceal deep drainage ditches — step carefully off the tarmac
